## Question

Informal urban morphology produces environmental conditions that are not well predicted by standard urban climate models. Streets are narrower, slopes steeper, buildings denser, and surfaces are heterogeneous in ways the literature has not measured at scale. The question this work asks: which morphological parameters of an informal settlement actually predict local airflow, thermal comfort, and solar exposure — and which are modifiable?

## Approach

The pipeline starts with LiDAR scans of the built form, runs CFD airflow simulations over the reconstructed geometry, and produces solar exposure maps at the same resolution. Each output is reduced to per-building and per-street metrics, then regressed against compact morphological descriptors (height-to-width ratio, sky-view factor, frontal area density, etc.). The point is to find the parameters that are both predictive and actionable — a metric that no one can change is information without leverage.

### Methods

- LiDAR ground capture across selected Rio favela sites
- CFD airflow simulation over reconstructed urban canyon geometry
- Solar exposure mapping, hourly, full year
- Morphological parameter extraction at building and block scale
- Regression of environmental signal against morphology

## Why this matters

Standard climate adaptation guidance assumes a planned street grid. In informal settlements that assumption fails — and the populations most exposed to environmental risk are precisely those the standard guidance does not describe. A morphology-first model gives local actors variables they can recognize on the ground.
